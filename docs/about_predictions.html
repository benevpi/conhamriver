How the predicted water quality works

Back to home page

Each report shows a predicted bathing-water quality — Excellent, Good, Sufficient or Poor — alongside the existing risk level. This page explains what that prediction means, how it is calculated, and (importantly) what it can and cannot tell you.

The categories

These are the UK inland bathing-water bands for E. coli (CFU/100 ml):

CategoryE. coli level
Excellent≤ 500
Good≤ 1000
Sufficient≤ 900 (90th percentile basis)
Poorexceeds the Sufficient level

Officially these bands are assessed from percentiles of a whole season's samples, not a single reading. Here we apply the same boundary values to a single predicted concentration, as a simplification, so the wording matches what people recognise.

How the prediction is made

The prediction is driven by the two things this tool can see in near real time:

  • Upstream storm-overflow (CSO) spilling — the total number of hours that monitored overflows upstream (within about 20 miles) have been spilling over the last 7 days.
  • Recent rainfall — roughly the last three days of rain at the site.

The category is then set as follows:

Upstream spilling (last 7 days)Base prediction
under 15 hoursExcellent
15 to 100 hoursGood
100 to 500 hoursSufficient
500 hours or morePoor

If there has also been heavy recent rain (20 mm or more in ~3 days), the prediction is bumped one category worse, to account for rainfall-driven contamination (agricultural and urban run-off) that the overflow data does not capture.

Why this model, and not a regression?

We looked hard at the historical data for Conham — 25 digitised E. coli samples from the 2025 sampling programme, matched against upstream CSO spill records (Wessex Water's Event Duration Monitoring) and daily rainfall. Two findings shaped the design:

  1. CSO spilling is the best available predictor, but the relationship is weak and noisy. Upstream spill hours beat rainfall, individual outfalls, and several other features in cross-validation — but the correlation is modest. Rainfall on its own adds almost nothing once spilling is known.
  2. A straight regression would be unsafe here. The lab caps readings at 1000, so the worst days are "censored", and with a weak signal a least-squares model pulls every estimate towards the average. Fitted honestly, it predicts "Excellent" for every day — including the days that actually hit the 1000 ceiling. A model that always says "fine" is worse than useless for a swimming warning.

So instead we use a precautionary rule: when there has been a lot of upstream spilling (and/or heavy rain), the prediction is deliberately cautious and moves towards Poor. It is tuned so that the genuinely bad historical days (heavy December spilling) come out as Sufficient or Poor. The cost is some false alarms — a few quiet-water days with lots of upstream spilling get flagged as worse than they measured — which is the safe direction to err for river swimming.

What it deliberately ignores

Three historical days had high E. coli with essentially no upstream spilling and little or no rain (26 Jul, 27 Sep and 22 Nov 2025). These look like an unmonitored source, a gap in the overflow data, or something non-sewage. They cannot be predicted from spills or rain, so they were excluded when calibrating the model. This means the prediction can miss a genuinely bad day that arrives with dry weather and no recorded spilling.

Important caveats

  • The model was calibrated on ~22 weekly samples at Conham only, then applied to the other sites on the assumption that upstream spilling affects them similarly. It has not been validated at those sites.
  • Weekly grab samples cannot resolve how quickly levels rise or fall within a few days, and the E. coli values were read off a published chart, so they are approximate and capped at 1000.
  • Rainfall is from a ~9 km weather model, not a gauge at the river.
  • "Upstream" and distances are crude geometric approximations, and the monitored overflows do not cover every possible source.
  • This is an experimental, precautionary estimate — not a measurement, and not a guarantee of safety. River swimming is never 100% safe; please make your own informed decision.
